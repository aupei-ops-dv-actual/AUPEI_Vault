5. Stability Analysis
The derivation in Section 3 establishes the geometric magnitude of the proton mass.
This section addresses the second half of the Mass Gap problem: the stability of that
mass. Why does the proton, uniquely among hadrons, refuse to decay?
5.1 The π 2 Floor: A Geometric Minimum
In standard particle physics, decay is a transition to lower-mass states. A particle is
stable if there are no lighter states available into which it can decay while conserving
fundamental quantum numbers (charge, baryon number, etc.).
The 7dU framework reframes this limit geometrically. Within the curvature-bound
(baryonic) sector of the collapse spectrum, π 2 is the first stable eigenvalue. There is no
stable geometric configuration below π 2 in this sector of the collapse operator’s
eigenvalue spectrum.
Configurations driven below the π 2 floor do not settle into a new bound state; they run
away into collapse (singular behavior dominated by ζ) or evaporate into non-baryonic
noise. In either case, the baryonic identity is lost rather than reshaped into a lighter
proton-like object. The proton is stable because it occupies the ground state of
connectivity—the lowest rung of the ladder available to baryonic matter.
5.2 Distinct Geometric Basins
The proton and electron occupy distinct geometric basins in the ζ,ξ,ω phase space:
• Proton: a ζ-dominated bound mode at the π 2 floor.
• Electron: a ξ-dominated drift mode near the π 2 + Δπ ceiling.
Transitioning between these basins requires crossing the energy gap Δπ —a barrier that
stabilizes the proton against decay. Metaphorically, the proton is like a “knot” in the
curvature field: a localized, self-sustaining configuration that cannot unwind without
accessing higher-energy states.
This geometric separation provides a natural explanation for the observed conservation
of baryon number in low-energy physics. While Grand Unified Theories propose high-
energy channels for proton decay (p → e + + π 0), 7dU predicts these channels are
suppressed by the geometric barrier associated with the π 2 floor.

5.3 Fortitude: The Balance of Forces
On the geometric side, the proton sits at the dynamical equilibrium point where the
collapse tendency driven by Constraint (ζ) is balanced by the expansion tendency
driven by Expansion (ω), under the stochastic pressure of Chance (ξ). Mathematically,
the proton corresponds to a fixed point where the constraint operator (−ζ∇ 2) and the
stochastic term (ξ𝒫), in the presence of ω, minimize the collapse operator eigenvalue
at λ = π 2.
We associate this balance with the virtue we call Fortitude: the ability of structure to
persist in the face of both chaos (ξ) and collapse (ζ). The proton is the first nontrivial
instance of that balance in the 7dU spectrum—a standing wave of Fortitude that holds
its shape against both dilution and implosion.
